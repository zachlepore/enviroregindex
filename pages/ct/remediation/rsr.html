Connecticut Remediation Quick Guide

Remediation Standard Regulations (RSRs)

Connecticut’s RSRs are the core cleanup standards used to evaluate whether soil and groundwater conditions are acceptable at a site. They establish the numeric criteria, exposure assumptions, and compliance pathways that environmental engineers, LEPs, and consultants rely on during investigation, remedy design, and closure.

Plain-English Overview

The RSRs are Connecticut’s foundational cleanup regulations for contaminated property. In practice, they tell project teams what concentrations in soil and groundwater can remain, what exposure scenarios matter, and what additional controls may be needed when a site cannot achieve unrestricted cleanup.

Engineers use the RSRs to compare investigation data against applicable criteria, determine whether a release has been adequately addressed, and support remedial decisions that can be documented in a verification or regulatory submittal.

Relationship to RBCRs

Connecticut’s Release-Based Cleanup Regulations (RBCRs), effective March 1, 2026, changed the program structure for reporting and managing releases, but they did not replace the technical role of the RSRs. The RSRs still function as the underlying cleanup standards used to evaluate soil and groundwater conditions.

A practical way to think about it is: RBCRs govern the release-based process and compliance framework, while the RSRs remain the core numeric and technical cleanup benchmark for many remediation decisions.

When Engineers Use This

  • During Phase II and Phase III investigation work to compare analytical results against Connecticut cleanup criteria.
  • When evaluating whether impacted soil can remain in place, requires removal, or needs an engineered control or land use restriction strategy.
  • When assessing groundwater exceedances, pollutant mobility, and whether long-term remediation or variance pathways may be needed.
  • When preparing LEP verification packages, closure documentation, remedial action plans, or agency-facing technical memoranda.

Common Questions

  • Are the RSRs still relevant after the RBCR rollout? Yes. They remain central to the technical cleanup analysis even though the broader program structure changed.
  • Do the RSRs only apply to soil? No. They address both soil and groundwater, including exposure and migration pathways.
  • Can a site move forward if it cannot meet every numeric criterion directly? Sometimes. Connecticut allows certain compliance mechanisms, including engineered controls and technical impracticability pathways, depending on site conditions and regulatory requirements.
